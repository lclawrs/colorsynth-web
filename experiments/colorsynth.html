THE COLORSYNTH ALGORITHM

Every pixel on screen maps to a point on the complex plane — a 2D number system where each point has a real and imaginary part. ColorSynth feeds that point through a mathematical formula (the variation), getting back a new complex number. The distance and angle of that result become the pixel's color.

The formulas use functions like sin, exp, and log applied to complex numbers — which produce wildly different geometry than their real-number equivalents. sin(z) for complex z wraps the entire plane into an infinite tiling of interlocked shapes.

WHY IT'S FAST — GLSL SHADERS

Your phone's GPU is running this — not the CPU. The math for every pixel runs simultaneously in a fragment shader: a tiny program written in GLSL (OpenGL Shading Language) that executes millions of times per frame, once per pixel, all in parallel.

A modern phone GPU has thousands of shader cores. A 1080×1920 screen is ~2 million pixels. Running serially that'd be impossible at 60fps — in parallel on the GPU it's trivial.

THE MATH — COMPLEX PLANE

Some of the patterns you're seeing have deep mathematical names. Penrose is a quasicrystal: five plane waves at 72° increments that never quite repeat — mathematically proven to be aperiodic. Wallpaper p6m is one of exactly 17 wallpaper symmetry groups that can tile the infinite plane. Escher Spiral uses logarithmic coordinates, the same geometry Escher used in his woodcuts.

The Julia set variation traces the boundary of a set defined by iterating z² + c — a fractal with infinite detail at every zoom level.
